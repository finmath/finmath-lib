15    /**
16    * The interface for a model of a stochastic process <i>X</i> where
17    * <i>X(t) = f(t,Y(t))</i> and <br>
18    * \[
19    * dY_{j} = \mu_{j} dt + \lambda_{1,j} dW_{1} + \ldots + \lambda_{m,j} dW_{m}
20    * \]
21    *
22    * <ul>
23    * <li>The value of <i>Y(0)</i> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getInitialState}.
24    * <li>The value of &mu; is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getDrift}.
25    * <li>The value &lambda;<sub>j</sub> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#getFactorLoading}.
26    * <li>The function <i>f</i> is provided by the method {@link net.finmath.montecarlo.model.ProcessModel#applyStateSpaceTransform}.
27    * </ul>
28    * Here, &mu; and &lambda;<sub>j</sub> may depend on <i>X</i>, which allows to implement stochastic drifts (like in a LIBOR market model)
29    * of local volatility models.
30    *
31    * <br>
32    * Examples:
33    * <ul>
34    * <li>
35    * The Black Scholes model can be modeled by S = X = Y (i.e. f is the identity)
36    * and &mu;<sub>1</sub> = r S and &lambda;<sub>1,1</sub> = &sigma; S.
37    * </li>
38    * <li>
39    * Alternatively, the Black Scholes model can be modeled by S = X = exp(Y) (i.e. f is exp)
40    * and &mu;<sub>1</sub> = r - 0.5 &sigma; &sigma; and &lambda;<sub>1,1</sub> = &sigma;.
41    * </li>
42    * </ul>
43    *
44    * @author Christian Fries
45    * @version 2.0
46    */
